The general idea

I’ve written dozens of sketches for the system, many with conflicting details, but I’ll try to convey the general idea.

The plastic extruder works by taking a large sample of plastic, often of irregular shape. A grinder mechanism then melts and cuts the plastic into smaller, roughly uniform pieces referred to as pellets. These pellets fall between the grooves of a rotating screw, where they are heated and pushed toward the end tip, which produces a line of plastic with a specific diameter. This extruded plastic is then rapidly cooled and wound onto a spool, continuing until the entire sample has been processed or the spool is full. Once fully cooled, the product is called filament, which can be used in various commercially available 3D printers.

Upon review, there are four general sections of the system:

1. The "Hotbox" (heat it and crush it)
2. The "Screw" (make it a line)
3. The "Cooler" (keep it that way)
4. The "Spooler" (wrap it up for storage)

The "hotbox" notes

I started by brainstorming different ways to melt the plastic. After a lot of testing, I decided to use thin nichrome wire.

Next, I considered how to support the metal wire using inexpensive materials without causing a short. One promising idea was to use nails, but as noted, this approach would only work if:

- The heat at the intersection of the print and the nail is less than the heat required over a long period that would deform the plastic.

- No “hot spots” or electrical shorts form due to the winding of the nail.

After a lot of planning and modeling, I settled on the following general configuration:

Two bases: Each with 25 screw holes, for a total of 50, shifted by 90 degrees so the nichrome wires cut different sections at each level.

IT WORKS!
..kinda

With a 600 W power dissipation and a wire length of 1540 mm, the nichrome wire could reach estimated temperatures in the range of 800 °C to 1000 °C.

During these tests, it successfully “sliced” several items, and once slightly cooled, they became extremely brittle and broke into pieces—ideally suitable for processing in the grinder.

Some issues did arise, but they can be addressed:

Wire slipping: The physical expansion of the wire from high heat caused it to slip.
(Fix: wind the wire at every nail point, not just the terminal ends.)

Bed deformation: Ambient heat caused the plastic bed of the hotbox to melt and bend inward.
(Fix: reduce slipping and reinforce the bed with additional material and support.)

Plastic smoke: Residue left over produced smoke.
(Fix: implement air extraction and filters.)

Wiring improvements

Ever since the start of the Hotbox, I have been burning through my alligator clips and the thin copper that connects them. I knew they were not rated for anything near 120 V, but they were all I had that suited the task at the time. After the 240 V test, it became clear that I really needed to upgrade. With no wire on hand capable of handling mains voltage, I had to get creative. I found a data cable containing eight copper leads, separated them into two at both ends, then soldered them and added old alligator clips. This gave me just enough wiring to safely handle mains voltage.

Initial cooler design

I started with a design featuring two pipes: one running to the top platform and the other to the base of the bottom. Each pipe was connected to its respective area of the pump. The goal was to create a consistent “overflow” effect from the platform, allowing the material to cool in a straight line without warping the final plastic.

Unfortunately, the design did not function as intended. With the output valve at the same level as the input, the pump did not have enough force—even at a stable 12 V—to properly circulate the water. Leaks were also frequent due to loose valves.

Photo of the new design

Cooler with improvements

While not perfect, significant progress was made with this new version. Instead of placing the output valve at the bottom, it has been shifted to the top, centered over the top platform. This change also improves print quality and creates a tighter seal. While some splashing and minor leaks still occur, these issues are greatly reduced compared to previous tests and can be further improved with better motor power control and additional iterations of the valve design until all components achieve a tight fit.

A "duck tape" extruder test

For heating, I plan to use two proper band heater elements that have yet to arrive. I also have not yet assembled the stand for the extruder, but I wanted to run a simple test to get an initial idea of how things would behave. I used some Kapton tape I had purchased for this project to create a simple winding of nichrome wire between two layers of tape around the pipe. To act as a “motor,” I used a simple drill with its chuck attached to the extruder screw. Of course, I knew this was unlikely to fully work, but I wanted to observe the results anyway. Here is what I found:

1. As expected, a single coil of nichrome was not nearly enough to fully melt the plastic.

2. The rotational force on the pipe, enabled by friction, was much higher than I anticipated—even when removing the wire leads at times. This emphasizes the need for a strong stand and proper accounting of forces.

3. The 1–2 mm difference in diameter between the pipe and the extruder did not appear to be a major issue, as I had feared, but it remains something to monitor in later testing.

SRR testing

The SSR, or solid-state relay, is a device that acts as an intermediary, allowing control of mains voltage from a low-power device, similar to a common MOSFET. By applying the positive and negative terminals of a DC power source to the input, we can allow power to flow through the Hotbox and provide the required heat. This power source can be any suitable DC supply, but in my case it will be controlled by a microcontroller (such as an Arduino) to automatically regulate temperature using PWM (pulse-width modulation).

Cooling peltiar

For cooling the water, I will be using a Peltier device, which, when voltage is applied across it, cools one face while heating the other. When combined with a small amount of thermal paste, a heat sink, and a fan to remove heat from the hot side, it can lower the water temperature by up to 30 °C below ambient (for example, a 20 °C room could be cooled to approximately –10 °C).

Spool holder (Drill chuck)

Typically, when using filament, a spool simply sits on a loose rod and is pulled by a stepper motor, slowly unwinding like a wheel on an old cassette tape. So, in theory, we could do the reverse: have a stepper motor push the filament forward so it wraps around an empty spool.

Unfortunately, it’s not that simple for several reasons:

Filament softness: Even after water cooling and exposure to air, the filament is likely still soft enough that it cannot be reliably pushed by gears.

Shape memory: Filament naturally wants to retain the curve it had when cooled on a spool. If you pick up a roll of filament and manipulate it, you’ll notice it tries to spring back into its original shape, much like a slinky toy. If you simply push a line of filament that is attached at one end, it will likely fall off the spool and collect in a messy heap at the bottom.

In a perfect setup, the spool itself would rotate slowly, allowing the slightly soft filament to collect on it while gradually cooling. This would result in a proper spool of filament that retains its natural arcing shape. My idea to achieve this is to use a drill chuck of some kind.

photo of some of the modeling process for the spool holder

Spool holder's eternal series of model adjustment

Starting from a simple drill chuck, there was a lot that had to be done to make it work the way I wanted. So much so that I even considered not calling it a chuck mechanism at all, as its function and purpose are quite different.

The three main goals were:

1. Allowing easy and automatic swapping of spools

2. Supporting at least 1 kg for its lifetime

3. Working with as many spool types as possible

The simplest way to think about how the chuck achieves this is to consider a typical drill chuck: as the second ring rotates, the three rails rotate inward and apply force evenly onto a drill bit. In my design, I am doing the same but applying the force outwards instead of inwards. While this sounds simple in theory, there is no magic “inverse mechanism” button, so it required a lot of trial and error in the modeling process—very reminiscent of the
Spray Assist project I did a while back.

Finally, I got it working, evenly applying force with no bending under a 1 kg load. This is by far one of the coolest mechanisms I’ve made to watch in action, and I can also see it being applied to other tasks, like rope or coil winding, either in companies or in my own future projects.

Thermocouple

The reason for the puddle of plastic observed in the two-extruder test is simply that the nozzle was heated above the temperature it needed to be. Of course, I anticipated this, so we need something to monitor and regulate the output of the band heaters. I decided to use a thermocouple.

The thermocouple I used is rated up to 1,000 °C, but since we only need roughly 200 °C, that limit is more than sufficient. Its job is simple: to maintain the temperature of the nozzle (not the entire system or the band heater itself) at a target temperature, which we’ll call X. While we don’t know the exact value of X yet, it will likely fall within the 150–250 °C range for most plastics. This value will also be software-defined, so we can adjust it as needed.

When the thermocouple reads a temperature above X, it will turn the band heater off; when the temperature falls below X, it will turn the heater back on. This creates a cycle of states, resulting in a roughly consistent temperature for the plastic.

Thermocouple upgrade

Using a one-ended nut, I welded it near the nozzle so that the thermocouple can screw into it and accurately measure the temperature at the nozzle. The next step is to leverage this more precise reading rather than simply taping the thermocouple to the nozzle to develop a proper control system. This system will maintain a consistent nozzle temperature by regulating the band heater through the use of the SSR (solid-state relay).
