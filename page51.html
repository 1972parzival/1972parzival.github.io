OpenSauce 2024

a recap and highlight reel of the coolest things i saw at opensauce

Self Driving Cars?

Before we even arrived at the convention, we were greeted by some amazing technology. On my way from the airport, I spotted several self-driving cars. These Waymo vehicles operate as a taxi service across parts of San Francisco—and don’t even have a person in the driver’s seat!

Is this really safe? Probably not, but to their credit, every time we saw one, it operated perfectly—even on some very challenging San Francisco crossings and hills. The sensors it uses to achieve this include LiDAR, cameras, and radar.

Metal Hand?

One really cool project I saw was a fully metal hand! Using an amazingly complex gear system, the hand provides an impressive amount of dexterity and flexibility, without requiring the user to charge it, making it practical for everyday use. The creator is working on producing the product commercially and currently has a few demo units in use by people with impairments, in order to gather feedback and improve the design over time.

Cad Software with Live Remote Control + Gpd win 2

met the man behind CommonWealth Robotics, Kevin Harrington, after spotting him holding a GPD Win 2 (an amazing portable computer) while walking through the conference center. It was awesome to talk to him about the device, and I was even more blown away by the software it was running!

"BowlerStudio by CommonWealth Robotics is a robot development app combining scripting, CAD, and simulation. It supports Java/Groovy, Python, and Clojure, with scripts from local storage or GitHub. Features include a kinematics engine, the MuJoCo Physics Engine for simulations, and a Creature Creator for generating 3D-printable robots."

It was amazing to see the software in action. I loved the features he demoed and even got to control the robot cat through the software while watching the simulated CAD model react in real time. I will certainly try to implement it into my workflow when I have more resources to invest in larger robotics projects.

Positron 3D printer


I got to see several Positron 3D printers in person and watched them print. I’ve been following the Positron project for several years, ever since it was created and showcased by kralyn3d. It was amazing to watch the whole video series and then see some printers in action, while also discussing interesting topics with the team—such as how the new clear heated bed system works.

The main goal of the Positron is to be as compact as possible while still maintaining a standard build plate size. It achieves this by printing “upside down”, which ensures a low center of mass during fast movements, and by using an innovative belt system. It’s a fascinating open-source project, and I look forward to seeing how the team progresses after the launch.

Micronics 3D

I got to see Micronics 3D, who built and launched a fully working desktop SLS 3D printer. It was fascinating to watch the demo items being produced and to learn about the manufacturing process, while also talking with the people behind this amazing project.

PillBot (endiatx)

I met the team behind the PillBot, a small, pill-sized device that contains a live camera system and a movement mechanism. It was fascinating to see how the device shrunk in size over time (see photo) and to learn about the challenges they had to overcome—such as designing a movement system that could unjam itself in tight spaces within the human body while avoiding any harm.

I also learned a lot about its potential impact on the medical industry, particularly in relation to gut health, and gained insight into the process of approving medical devices for public use, including the stage the team is currently at in that process.

Framework team


I got to meet the Framework team, and it was amazing to see a project with such a respectable goal and the team behind it in person. Framework is a laptop manufacturer focused on repairability, upgradability, and customization, all while maintaining a modern design.

The team was great to talk to, and I had a fantastic discussion about how they have leveraged largely unpaid social media to grow their business. I also shared how I discovered the project through LTT and Louis Rossman. The experience was incredible—they seemed to really enjoy talking with me and even gave me a free hat, which they signed for me!

Colorado PCB

I also got to meet the family-run team behind Colorado PCB. They were a joy to talk to, and I really enjoyed discussing their PCB manufacturing process, including etching, plating, and engraving. We also talked about my thoughts on how the industry might shift in the coming years, which they seemed to firmly agree with.

I really appreciated the family-run atmosphere of the business and have kept in contact with them via email. I hope that one day I can visit their plant in Colorado to see how everything works firsthand.

Final Thoughts

Overall, OpenSauce 2024 was amazing. Whether it was meeting incredible startup founders and discussing their journeys, or seeing content creators I’ve followed for years, the experience was truly inspiring.

When you’re working on projects—especially solo—it’s easy to feel isolated. Events like this really highlight how many other people like you are out there, all working on their own projects and building cool things. I hope I can afford to come back next year and experience it all over again!
