Bike bag

The Issue

I ride my electric bike to school everyday, i love it.
there is however one small issue.
carrying around my textbooks, iPad and notebooks all day is a pain.
When I ride around on the weekends running errands getting anything that can't fit in my pockets is a pain.

The goal of this project is to build a rack that goes over the back wheel of my bike that i can hook my bag onto, so I can carry heavy loads safely.

Version 0.1  

I’m starting by creating the “sheath,” which is essentially a tube that wraps snugly around the bike seat. This will serve as a solid anchor point for building the rest of the system.

Version 0.2 and Version 0.3

I discovered that trying to wedge the bottom of the sheath into the gap between the seat and the bike frame didn’t fit at all. To fix this, I switched to a more traditional approach: I modeled a system where bolts screw into horizontally mounted nuts, creating a strong “gripping” force around the bike seat.

Version 0.4

After making some slight adjustments to the model and tweaking the infill, I ended up with a sheath that wraps around the bike seat securely without snapping.

Version 0.5

With a solid sheath as the foundation, I moved on to modeling the main “body” of the rack. Since it was twice the maximum Z height of my P1P printer (250 mm), I had to split it into two halves. I used bolts in the middle to align the pieces precisely, then carefully screwed and glued them together for a secure assembly.

PLA is not steel

This is a fact i seem to commonly forget. The main "body" of the rack was printed in two pieces and then connected by bolts, then superglued together along the seem. After some basic testing with gym free weights and rough calculations, I realized it couldn’t reliably support my backpack, let alone heavier loads. While I did find a metal rack that was strong enough, it didn’t fit the bike stem, so my efforts weren’t entirely wasted.

Version 0.6

The only modification to part 0.4 was the addition of a long stem that attaches to the main sheath. At the end of this stem is a horizontal slot where a 5 mm bolt slides through and is secured with a nut on the opposite side. The same 5 mm bolt-and-nut configuration is also used to attach the bottom of the rack to the bike frame at two points.
