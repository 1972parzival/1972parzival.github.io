Understanding Gears

Through the lens of math

Notebook Scan

Initial thoughts 

I was merely sitting down thinking about gears—especially circular gears—and wondering how they came into being and how they were first modeled. The purpose of this post is to attempt recreating a method to create gears using only simple programming tools like arrays and basic mathematics. I am deliberately not researching how gears were historically made or leveraging AI for guidance, as that would defeat the spirit of the challenge. Now, to break down my thoughts on the subject.

A gear, in the simplest sense, is just a function of gaps and material that link together. Shown in the top left is a roughly half section of zero height, with the second half consisting of a linear rise, a flat top, and a linear fall. I have termed this a “gearic cycle.” One way to think of it is as the default state—like in a straight gear, where this gearic cycle simply repeats along its length.

When we consider a circle, the gearic cycle can be thought of as applied along a circle of infinite radius. At infinite radius, the distance between points A and B is at its maximum value. As the radius decreases from infinity, the distance between A and B decreases proportionally to the arc. This assumes the size of the gearic cycle remains constant and does not scale directly as the radius decreases.

In terms of equations, we know that the circumference of a circle is:

C = 2(pi)R.

Logically, the length of a single gearic cycle divided by the circumference of the circle gives the maximum number of possible cycles:

C/L=N (or C/N=L)

Therefore

n= (2(pi)R)/L and R = (NL)/2(pi)

From this, we can see that the ratio between gears is fundamentally determined by the difference in the number of cycles they contain. This allows us to estimate the radii of two circles, each with a cycle length of 1, that would result in a 20:1 gear ratio (as shown in the picture).
