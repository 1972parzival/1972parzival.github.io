Making a Piezoelectric

The Atomic Spring

The Atomic Spring

One way to explain piezoelectrics that I haven’t seen elsewhere is the idea that piezos are, in fact, all springs. If you have a standard metal spring and you compress it, you turn force into elastic energy. Similarly, if you have a piezo and you compress it, you turn force into electrical energy. This process also works in reverse: if you apply electrical energy, you can generate force—not a lot of force comparatively, but it comes in the form of vibration.

So, if we can create vibration based on an electrical signal, we have essentially invented the speaker. And if we can detect vibration and convert it back into an electrical signal, we have invented the microphone. These are the two applications I will focus on, though there are many others. For example, applying a lot of force all at once in a small spot can create short arcs of high-voltage AC—a technique called piezoelectric ignition, which we explored during the RIS project.

Intro and Setup

For this project, I will be attempting to make a piezoelectric crystal from scratch.

This can be done using relatively easy-to-acquire materials, maybe not ones you already have in your cabinets, but a short trip to the store should give you more than what you need:

Soda ash
Cream of tar tar
Water
a basic filter of some kind
a Plastic curing container
a glass or metal can 
a stove with a pot that will fit your can

Results

Overall, this reaction is extremely slow. It took me about an hour of slowly adding soda ash in small portions, waiting for the bubbles to settle, and repeating the process until the solution became clear with a light yellow hue.

After about four hours, I began to see crystal formation near the top layer of the liquid.

After approximately twelve hours, solid crystals had formed at the bottom of the plastic container.

Finally, after forty-eight hours, I drained the crystal solution and left the crystals to dry for another forty-eight hours to fully harden.
