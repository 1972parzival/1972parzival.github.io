DIY Pressure Containers

create your containers that can store pressure for fun projects at home

THIS PROJECT USES RESIN CURING

"Liquid 3D printing resins give off fumes or vapors containing volatile organic compounds (VOCs) that can be harmful to humans if inhaled into the throat or lungs. Short-term exposure to resin fumes can result in dizziness, headaches, and throat irritation"

Use the protective equipment mentioned in the materials section, or proceed at your own risk

Materials 

For this project I will converting two bottles into containers that can properly hold pressure (tested up to ~10 PSI). 
one smaller bottle will not have a exit valve, while the larger soda bottle will have a barbed exit connecter.

to do this you I used:

general:

1 large soda bottle
1 small water bottle
1 air compressor (manual or automatic)
1 3d printed barbed exit cap
1 ball pump needle

sealants: 
super glue (larger gaps)
UV resin (smaller air tight gaps)

safety equipment (if using UV resin):

nitrile gloves 
half-face respirator


Sealing: Super Glue

To fill the larger gaps, I used super glue. This was particularly useful because it creates a thicker barrier and can seal regions where UV resin wouldn’t properly cure especially in areas not directly exposed to light.

The process was simple: I unscrewed the cap, applied a steady layer of super glue around the bottle’s threads while rotating it, and then quickly tightened the cap back on. After sealing, I left it to cure for at least 24 hours to ensure a strong and airtight bond.

photo of the resin used, paintbrush and box for the resin

Sealing: UV Resin

Next, I used UV resin to seal the smaller gaps. The resin I used came straight from my resin 3D printer, but most UV-curable resins or airtight sealants will work just as well for this purpose.

To apply it, I used an old paintbrush and carefully brushed a thin layer of resin around the edges of the bottle cap threads while slowly rotating the bottle. This ensured an even coat and minimized the chance of air pockets forming before curing.

photo of 2 black-lights aimed while curing the large soda bottle

UV Curing 

The bottles were then cured for a total of about 20 minutes, rotating them 90 degrees halfway through to ensure even exposure and hardening. Continue this process until the resin is completely cured and no longer tacky to the touch.

After that, I recommend leaving the bottles outside in direct sunlight for a full day. This helps guarantee that any remaining uncured resin fully hardens, creating a strong, airtight, and pressure-resistant seal.

Results

After the curing process, I tested both bottles using an air pump while carefully monitoring the pressure. It’s important to take your time during this step apply pressure gradually and pause between each pump to make sure the PSI remains stable and doesn’t drop from a slow leak.

If you notice a gradual loss of pressure or a sudden failure of the seal, don’t worry just repeat the sealing process. Since all the materials used are inexpensive and easy to work with, redoing a step or two isn’t a big deal.

Once your seal holds steady and the bottle maintains pressure, you’re ready to move on to fun experiments and small-scale projects involving compressed air like bottle rockets, pneumatic toys, or simple pressure-driven motors!
