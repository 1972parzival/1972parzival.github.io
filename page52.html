3D printed belt sander

This project is a follow up to the TPU BELT in attempt to adapt that system for a homemade belt sander

Photo of the V1

V1

This initial version was certainly a “throwing stuff at the wall to see what sticks” kind of prototype. I just wanted to model some components, print them, and observe what broke.

One issue you can see in the photo—and definitely in the demo videos—is that there are significant problems with belt oscillation. Several factors contribute to this:

Inconsistent threading on the motor, which causes the nut to sit at a slight angle that is amplified over the connector.

The motor is supported by only two anchor points, causing it to lean slightly over the base at an upward angle.

The ball bearing side is unweighted, allowing vibrations from rotation and the “pulling” forces of the belt.

The belt slipping and shifting multiple times during demos.

New Motor Connector System

After realizing that the motor’s thread had a specific shape, I decided to use this to improve the installation process and eliminate the need for a nut. This change also reduced the oscillation issues seen in version 1, which were caused by inconsistent threading. I quickly modeled and printed a test piece (shown to the left) and was overjoyed to find that it worked!

photo of V2

V2

Overall, this new connection system had a huge effect on reducing the issues with belt oscillation. Another improvement in this version was the addition of small “railings” for the belt, ensuring it doesn’t slip off during use.

Some problems still remain: the ball bearing side isn’t weighted down, which allows it to flex and vibrate, and the joint along the bearing keeps shifting out over time, eventually falling out.

V3 (cooling system)

V3 adds a full cooling system. After several long-term stress tests on the motor and setup of V2, I discovered that the motor heats up rapidly. This issue is severe enough to risk both motor damage and potential burns to the user.

The cooling system is simple, consisting of just a fan blade. The fan is powered by the existing rotational force from the motor, transmitted through the connection system using two 90-degree gears along with bolts and threads.

This version took longer to develop than previous versions due to the added complexity and the need for dozens of test prints. These tests ensured the gears were properly aligned and that the grinder would have a longer lifespan.

Right is initial part then moves counter clockwise till final part, bottom

V4
(belt sizing and final adjustments)

The sanding belt I am using is approximately 18.8 mm in width. This means that the areas where the belt rotates must have matching gear pieces and TPU belt sections in order to create a proper sander.

To achieve this, the belt areas of both sections are lined up, and the overall length is adjusted based on the ratio between the total length and the gear area length. Any extra length from both parts is then removed.

Regarding the belt itself, we can calculate the required length using the circumference, cut it to size, and lightly glue the ends together.

V4

I finished testing the cooling system and then moved on to printing what I hope will be the final required parts, including a base with extended height and high infill density to ensure strength. During testing, this setup worked exceptionally well after press-fitting the motor using a simple clamp.

Update feb 2025

This was one of those “close but not quite there” projects, and I wanted to return with the improved design, engineering, and modeling skills I have now to finally finish the job. A system like this—which can create fully working machines, such as a grinder, using only a motor—is an important project that I believe could help a lot of people.

Image Description

CHUCK IT

Revising the project, the main problems that still exist are belt oscillation which, while reduced in past versions, is still too high for a proper product and the strength of the ball bearing side’s belt attachment, which tends to split off from its thread.

If only there were a mechanical system that allows for centering and tightening a rod while still being able to rotate… well, there is: my close friend, the drill chuck. If the chuck’s body were grooved for the belt, we could simply push it in, tighten it, and then place the belt over it with very little oscillation.

Conclusion
